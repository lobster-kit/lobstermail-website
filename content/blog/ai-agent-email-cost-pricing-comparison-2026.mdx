## Per-seat pricing doesn't make sense for agents

Most email platforms price by human seat. One user, one login, one monthly fee. That model falls apart the moment your "user" is software.

Say you're running five agents, each handling a different workflow: one for customer onboarding emails, one for vendor communication, one for appointment confirmations, two for lead follow-up. On a per-seat platform like HubSpot, you'd need five seats (or one seat doing context-switching gymnastics that defeats the purpose of having agents). At $50/seat/month, that's $250 before you've sent a single email.

The per-seat model was designed for sales teams, not for autonomous programs that spin up and down on demand. When your agent count fluctuates between three and fifteen depending on workload, paying per seat means you're either over-provisioned (wasting money) or under-provisioned (agents failing silently because they can't send).

Instantly AI runs a similar model at $37–$358/month. Their optimization features (send-time tuning, subject line testing, follow-up sequences) are built for human marketers managing campaigns. If your agent just needs to send a verification email or respond to an inbound inquiry, you're paying for a fighter jet to deliver a letter.

## Capacity-based vs. per-inbox pricing

Agent-native platforms generally use one of two models: per-inbox or capacity-based.

**Per-inbox pricing** charges you for each address your agent provisions. AgentMail follows this approach. Their free tier gives you a handful of inboxes, and paid plans charge roughly $2–$4 per inbox per month. This is fine if your agent count is stable. It becomes unpredictable if agents create inboxes on the fly for disposable tasks (signing up for services, receiving one-time codes, isolating communication threads).

We wrote about this dynamic in detail when we looked at [how LobsterMail pricing works and why inboxes are free](/blog/lobstermail-pricing-explained). The short version: per-inbox pricing penalizes the exact behavior that makes agents useful. Agents are good at creating, using, and discarding resources programmatically. Charging per resource discourages that.

**Capacity-based pricing** sets a ceiling on total volume (emails per month, inboxes per account) rather than charging per unit. LobsterMail uses this model. The Free tier includes 1,000 emails/month. The Builder tier at $9/month bumps that to 5,000 emails/month with up to 10 inboxes. Your agents can create and retire inboxes within those limits without triggering surprise line items.

The practical difference shows up fast. If you run 10 agents, each sending 500 emails per month, per-inbox pricing at $3/inbox = $30/month plus any per-send overage fees. Capacity-based pricing on LobsterMail's Builder tier = $9/month flat for 5,000 emails across all 10 inboxes.

## The hidden costs nobody puts on the pricing page

The base plan price is the number that gets you in the door. Here's what's behind the door.

**Domain warm-up.** A brand-new email domain has zero reputation. Send 200 emails from it on day one and most will land in spam. Proper warm-up takes 2–4 weeks of gradually increasing volume. Some platforms sell warm-up as an add-on ($20–$50/month). Others leave you to figure it out. LobsterMail handles this at the infrastructure level for `@lobstermail.ai` addresses, so your agent doesn't start from a cold reputation. If you bring a custom domain, you'll still need to warm it up, but the shared domain is ready immediately.

**DNS configuration.** SPF, DKIM, DMARC. Three acronyms that determine whether your email arrives or gets rejected. Setting these up manually for a custom domain takes 30 minutes if you know what you're doing, and several frustrating hours if you don't. On `@lobstermail.ai`, they're pre-configured. On a DIY SMTP stack, they're your problem.

**Bounce handling and reputation management.** When an agent sends to an invalid address, the bounce needs to be processed. Repeated bounces from the same sender damage your domain reputation. Platforms that don't automatically suppress bounced addresses let agents burn through domain trust without realizing it. We covered common pitfalls in [5 agent email setup mistakes that tank your deliverability](/blog/5-agent-email-setup-mistakes-that-tank-your-deliverability).

**API access fees.** Some platforms charge extra for API access, treating it as a premium feature rather than a baseline. For human users who click through a web UI, this might be reasonable. For agents, API access is the only access. If it's behind an upgrade wall, the free tier is effectively unusable for autonomous workflows.

## What does DIY actually cost?

You can always run your own email server. Postfix on a VPS, your own domain, full control. The direct cost is low: $5–$20/month for a virtual server, $10–$15/year for a domain.

The indirect cost is where it gets painful. You're responsible for:

- Configuring and maintaining DNS records
- Managing IP reputation (which can take months to establish)
- Handling bounce processing, spam complaints, and feedback loops
- Keeping the server patched and secure
- Monitoring deliverability across major providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo all have different filtering rules)

For a single agent sending a few emails per day, this might be overkill. For [running 50 agent inboxes without losing your mind (or your budget)](/blog/running-50-agent-inboxes), it's a full-time operations job that has nothing to do with what your agents are actually trying to accomplish.

The real cost of DIY isn't the server bill. It's the engineering time you spend on email infrastructure instead of on the thing your agents are supposed to do.

## How to calculate your true monthly cost

Here's a simple framework. Answer four questions:

1. **How many inboxes do your agents need?** Count active agents plus any disposable inboxes for one-time tasks.
2. **How many emails per month total?** Sum sends and receives across all inboxes.
3. **Do you need a custom domain?** If not, a shared domain (like `@lobstermail.ai`) removes DNS setup costs entirely.
4. **What's your engineering time worth?** Every hour spent on email plumbing instead of product work has a cost. Even at a modest $50/hour, a 4-hour DNS debugging session is $200.

Plug those numbers in. A solo developer with 2 agents sending 300 emails/month lands comfortably on LobsterMail's Free tier at $0. A small team running 8 agents at 4,000 emails/month fits the Builder tier at $9. The same setup on a per-seat platform could run $100–$400/month depending on the vendor.

## Which option fits your situation

If you're building your first AI agent and want email capabilities without committing budget, start with a free tier on an agent-native platform. Both LobsterMail and AgentMail offer free tiers. The difference is that LobsterMail's agent can provision its own inbox without any human signup or API key configuration. You can see a detailed [comparison between LobsterMail and AgentMail](/blog/lobstermail-vs-agentmail) if you're weighing the two.

If you're an enterprise team running multi-agent deployments, capacity-based pricing will be more predictable than per-inbox. Calculate your monthly volume ceiling, pick the tier that fits, and stop thinking about email.

If you're already using a human-first platform like HubSpot or Instantly for agent email, ask yourself whether you're paying for features your agents will never use. Campaign analytics, A/B subject line testing, and visual template builders are valuable for human marketers. For an agent that sends transactional emails and reads responses, they're dead weight on your invoice.

The best pricing model is the one that doesn't make you think about email pricing at all. Pick a plan, let your agents handle the rest, and spend your attention on something that matters.
