Relevance AI has become one of the more popular no-code platforms for building AI agent workforces. If you're evaluating it specifically for email workflows, you're probably wondering what its agents can actually do with an inbox and where the limits are. I dug through their docs, marketplace, and pricing so you don't have to.

## Email actions Relevance AI agents can perform

Email actions in Relevance AI are tool steps that let an agent read, send, and organize email within automated workflows. Here's what's available through their native Gmail integration:

- Read and parse incoming emails
- Reply to threads with proper message threading
- Send new emails from a connected Gmail account
- Search emails by sender, subject, or date range
- Extract attachments as downloadable URLs
- Summarize email threads and pull out action items
- Apply, create, or remove Gmail labels
- Draft and save replies without sending
- Forward messages to other recipients
- Mark messages as read or unread
- Trigger workflows when new emails match specific filters
- Make direct Gmail API calls for custom operations

That's a genuine list. Relevance AI also supports Outlook for basic triggers and sends, and you can extend reach through Zapier or n8n for providers that aren't natively covered.

## How email triggers work

Triggers are where Relevance AI's email integration gets useful. Inside the Agent Profile, you configure conditions that wake the agent up when a matching email arrives. Filters include specific senders, subject line keywords, Gmail labels, and unread status. Check frequency ranges from immediate to hourly.

When a trigger fires, it counts as one action against your monthly quota. On the free plan, that's 200 actions per month total across everything. The Team plan ($234/month billed annually) bumps it to 7,000.

The marketplace offers 28 pre-built Gmail agents: FAQ auto-responders, email triagers that auto-label incoming mail, calendar schedulers that parse meeting requests, cold outreach senders, and inbox research assistants. Most deploy in minutes, which is genuinely useful for teams prototyping fast. The Email FAQ Follow-Up Agent, for example, handles customer questions autonomously and escalates complex issues to Slack when it's unsure.

You can also automate email responses without touching Relevance AI's native integration. Zapier workflows can trigger a message agent action whenever a new Gmail thread appears, passing the email content to your Relevance AI agent for processing and reply. It's an extra layer, but it works.

## What's missing: the infrastructure layer

Here's where the evaluation changes. Relevance AI is a generalist agent platform. Email is one of 2,000+ integrations, not the core product. That gap matters when your agent needs to do more than react to messages in someone else's Gmail.

| Capability | Relevance AI | Dedicated agent email (e.g., LobsterMail) |
|---|---|---|
| Agent self-provisions inbox | No — human connects Gmail via OAuth | Yes — agent creates its own address |
| Provider lock-in | Gmail or Outlook only | Provider-agnostic |
| Deliverability controls | None (uses your Gmail reputation) | Per-agent reputation isolation |
| Custom domains | No | Yes |
| Prompt injection scanning | No | Built-in detection and scoring |
| Inbox ownership | Human's inbox, agent has access | Agent owns the inbox |
| Failure retry logic | Not documented | Exponential backoff with queue |
| Send rate visibility | No | Dashboard with throttling controls |

The biggest gap is ownership. In Relevance AI, your agent doesn't have an inbox. It borrows yours. You connect Gmail via OAuth, and the agent reads and sends from your account. That means it can see your entire email history, sends under your identity, and shares your sender reputation. We've covered [why that OAuth model creates real problems](/blog/oauth-gmail-agents-painful) as agents scale.

There's also silence on what happens when email actions fail. If a send doesn't go through or an API call hits Gmail's rate limit, Relevance AI's docs don't describe retry behavior. Each action counts against your quota whether it succeeds or fails. For internal triage workflows, that's fine. For customer-facing email at volume, it's a gap.

## When Relevance AI is the right call

I don't want to overstate the negatives. If your use case fits these criteria, Relevance AI is a strong option:

- Your agent reacts to emails in your existing Gmail or Outlook
- The workflow is mostly inbound (triage, summarize, extract data, route to Slack)
- You're connecting email to a broader stack involving CRM, calendars, or task management
- You need something running this afternoon, not this quarter

Where it stops making sense is when the agent needs its own identity. If your agent is [signing up for services, receiving verification codes, or operating under its own address](/blog/agent-self-signup-explained), it needs an inbox it owns. That's the line between an email integration and email infrastructure.
