Last month a founder in our community lost 36 hours of inbound leads because their email migration "took five minutes" according to the vendor's docs. The MX records propagated unevenly, half the messages bounced, and the old provider had already stopped accepting mail. Thirty-six hours. For a two-person startup, that's not an inconvenience. That's revenue gone.

Zero downtime email migration sounds like a marketing promise. It's not. It's an engineering discipline with specific steps, and skipping any of them turns "zero" into "several hours of panic."

## Three migration strategies, ranked by risk

Not every migration looks the same. The right approach depends on how many mailboxes you're moving and how much complexity you're willing to manage.

**Cutover migration** moves everything at once. You point MX records to the new provider, and the old one stops receiving mail. This works for small teams (under 50 mailboxes) where you can coordinate a maintenance window. The risk is binary: either it works or everyone's email is down simultaneously.

**Staged migration** moves users in batches. You migrate 10-20% at a time, validate each batch, then proceed. This reduces blast radius but requires dual-delivery routing for the transition period, which adds operational overhead.

**Parallel-run migration** keeps both providers fully live. Mail routes to both systems simultaneously until you're confident the new one is solid, then you cut over. This is the safest option and the most complex. It's also the only strategy that genuinely achieves zero downtime for organizations with more than a few hundred mailboxes.

For agent-based email infrastructure, the calculus is different. When your agent provisions its own inbox through an API (the way [agent self-signup](/blog/agent-self-signup-explained) works with LobsterMail), there's no mailbox migration at all. The agent creates a new address on the new provider and starts using it. Old addresses can run in parallel until traffic drains naturally.

## How to migrate email infrastructure with zero downtime

1. Lower your MX record TTL to 300 seconds at least 48 hours before the migration starts.
2. Run IMAP sync (or native migration tooling) to pre-populate destination mailboxes with historical mail.
3. Execute a pilot migration covering 5-10% of your users and monitor for delivery failures.
4. Enable dual-delivery routing so both the old and new providers receive inbound mail simultaneously.
5. Switch MX records to point exclusively to the new provider.
6. Monitor bounce rates, delivery latency, and SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass rates for 24-48 hours.
7. Decommission the source provider only after confirming zero delivery anomalies.

Each step has a specific reason. Skip the TTL change and your cutover takes hours instead of minutes because DNS caches hold stale records. Skip the pilot and you discover that your shared mailboxes don't migrate cleanly, but only after 400 people can't receive email.

## The DNS cutover is where most teams get burned

MX records tell the world where to deliver your mail. When you change them, every mail server on the internet needs to pick up the update. That's not instant. It depends on TTL values, caching behavior of upstream resolvers, and whether the sending server retries or bounces on failure.

Here's what actually happens during a cutover: some senders see the new MX immediately. Others hold the cached old record for minutes or hours. If your old provider stops accepting mail before propagation completes, those messages bounce. They don't queue. They don't retry. They're gone.

The fix is dual-delivery. Configure your old provider to forward inbound mail to the new one (or use a routing service that delivers to both). Keep this running for at least 72 hours after the MX switch. I've seen propagation take that long in edge cases with aggressive caching.

## DKIM, DMARC, and the authentication gap nobody warns you about

Changing providers means changing the servers that sign your outbound mail. If your DKIM keys don't match what's published in DNS, receiving servers will flag your messages as suspicious or reject them outright.

Before migration day, publish DKIM records for the new provider alongside the existing ones. DNS supports multiple DKIM selectors, so both can coexist. Run test sends from the new infrastructure and verify DKIM alignment using a tool like [mail-tester.com](https://www.mail-tester.com/) or Google's postmaster tools. Only remove the old DKIM selector after decommissioning the old provider.

DMARC policy is the other gotcha. If you're running `p=reject`, any mail sent from the new infrastructure before DKIM is properly configured will be rejected by the recipient. Consider temporarily relaxing to `p=none` during migration, then tightening back once everything aligns.

## What happens to emails sent during migration?

This is the question that keeps IT leads up at night. During a well-executed parallel-run migration, the answer is: nothing bad. Both systems receive mail. The old system forwards what it catches to the new one. Messages arrive in both places, and you deduplicate on the new system using Message-ID headers.

During a cutover migration, the answer depends on timing. Messages sent in the gap between stopping the old provider and DNS propagation completing will bounce. That's why cutover migrations should only happen during low-traffic windows with pre-lowered TTLs.

For agent infrastructure, the problem is simpler. If your agent manages its own inboxes (whether through [self-hosted or managed infrastructure](/blog/self-hosted-vs-managed-agent-email)), migration means creating new inboxes on the new provider and updating the agent's configuration. The agent can monitor both old and new inboxes during transition using [webhooks or polling](/blog/webhooks-vs-polling-email), then phase out the old addresses once traffic shifts.

## Monitoring during and after migration

You need real-time visibility into four metrics during any migration:

- **Bounce rate** on outbound sends (should stay under 2%)
- **Delivery latency** for inbound mail (any spike over 30 seconds warrants investigation)
- **SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass rate** on outbound mail (should be 100% from day one)
- **Queue depth** on the old provider (should trend to zero within 72 hours)

Set up alerts before the migration starts, not during. If you're scrambling to configure monitoring while messages are in flight, you've already lost the plot.

## The real zero downtime strategy

Here's what I've learned from watching dozens of teams migrate email infrastructure: the teams that achieve zero downtime are the ones that spent the most time on pre-migration validation and the least time on the actual cutover.

Lower your TTLs early. Pre-sync your mailboxes. Run a pilot. Set up dual-delivery. Publish DKIM records for both providers. Configure monitoring. Then, when you flip the MX records, it's anticlimactic. That's the goal. A boring migration is a successful migration.

And if you're building agent infrastructure from scratch, consider whether migration is even the right framing. An agent that provisions its own inbox on demand doesn't need to migrate. It just starts using the new provider. That's the advantage of treating email as API-driven infrastructure instead of a monolithic service you move between vendors.

<FAQ>
  <FAQItem question="What does 'zero downtime' actually mean in the context of email infrastructure migration?">
    It means no period where inbound or outbound email fails to deliver. Messages sent during the transition should arrive without bouncing, delays beyond normal delivery times, or authentication failures. In practice, this requires dual-delivery routing during the cutover window.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="What is the difference between a cutover migration, a staged migration, and a parallel-run migration?">
    Cutover moves all mailboxes at once in a single maintenance window. Staged moves users in batches over days or weeks. Parallel-run keeps both providers fully live simultaneously, routing mail to both until the new one is validated. Risk decreases as complexity increases.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How do MX record TTL settings affect downtime risk during an email migration?">
    High TTL values (like the default 3600 seconds or higher) mean DNS caches hold old MX records longer after you switch. Lower your TTL to 300 seconds at least 48 hours before migration so the change propagates quickly once you flip records.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How long should you run dual-delivery before completing the final MX cutover?">
    At minimum 72 hours after the MX switch. Some upstream DNS resolvers cache aggressively, and you want to ensure every sender worldwide has picked up the new records before decommissioning the old provider.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How do you prevent duplicate emails during a parallel-run migration?">
    Deduplicate on the destination system using the Message-ID header, which is unique per email. Most enterprise mail platforms support deduplication rules, or you can filter duplicates at the application layer.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How do DKIM and DMARC records need to be updated during a migration to avoid mail being marked as spam?">
    Publish DKIM keys for the new provider alongside existing ones before migration. Both selectors can coexist in DNS. Consider relaxing your DMARC policy to `p=none` during transition, then restore `p=reject` after confirming alignment on the new infrastructure.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="What monitoring metrics should you watch in real time during an email infrastructure migration?">
    Track outbound bounce rate (keep under 2%), inbound delivery latency, SPF/DKIM/DMARC pass rates on outbound mail, and queue depth on the old provider. Set alerts before migration day, not during.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="Can an API-driven migration tool eliminate the need for scheduled maintenance windows?">
    Yes. When email infrastructure is API-driven, agents can provision new inboxes on the new provider and monitor both old and new addresses simultaneously. There's no hard cutover required because the agent manages the transition programmatically.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="What is the recommended rollback plan if a zero downtime migration encounters an unexpected failure?">
    Keep your old provider active and ready to receive mail for the full duration of the transition. If something breaks, revert MX records to the old provider. With a 300-second TTL, rollback takes about five minutes to propagate to most resolvers.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How do you migrate shared mailboxes, distribution lists, and aliases without disrupting delivery?">
    Migrate shared mailboxes and distribution lists in the pilot batch so you catch permission and routing issues early. Aliases should be recreated on the new provider before the MX switch. Test by sending to every alias from an external account.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How long does a typical enterprise email migration take end to end?">
    For a staged migration of 500+ mailboxes, expect 2-4 weeks including planning, pilot, batch migration, and post-migration monitoring. The actual MX cutover takes minutes. Everything else is preparation and validation.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="What is IMAP sync and when should it be used instead of native migration tools?">
    IMAP sync copies mail between providers using the IMAP protocol. Use it when migrating between different platforms (e.g., on-premises Exchange to Google Workspace) where native tools aren't available. Native migration tools are faster when both source and destination support them.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="What pilot migration size is recommended before a full enterprise email rollout?">
    Start with 5-10% of users, ideally from a mix of departments. Include at least one shared mailbox and one distribution list. Run the pilot for 48-72 hours and validate delivery, calendar sync, and contact migration before proceeding.
  </FAQItem>

  <FAQItem question="How do you validate that no emails were lost after migration completes?">
    Compare message counts between source and destination for a sample of mailboxes. Send test emails from external accounts during and after migration. Check bounce logs on both providers. Monitor your postmaster dashboard for delivery anomalies for at least one week post-migration.
  </FAQItem>
</FAQ>
