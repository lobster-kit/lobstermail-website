Sometime in the last six months, a line was quietly crossed. More AI agents signed up for email addresses than human freelancers did. Nobody published a press release about it. There was no conference talk. But if you run email infrastructure (we do), you can see it in the data: agent-provisioned inboxes are growing faster than human ones, and the gap is accelerating.

So what happens next? Not in five years. In the next eighteen months. By mid-2027, agent email will look different from what we have today in ways that matter for anyone building, deploying, or relying on AI agents. Here are five predictions I'm genuinely confident about and a couple I'm less sure of.

## 1. Most transactional email will be agent-to-agent

Right now, agents send email to humans: verification codes, status updates, outreach messages. The human reads it (or ignores it). But we're already seeing a second pattern emerge. Agents emailing other agents. One agent sends a purchase order. Another agent receives it, parses it, confirms availability, and replies with an invoice. No human touches the thread.

By 2027, I expect agent-to-agent email to surpass agent-to-human email in raw volume for transactional use cases. This isn't a wild guess. CB Insights [reported](https://www.cbinsights.com/research/ai-agent-predictions-2026/) that enterprises are already architecting multi-agent systems where communication layers are a first-class concern. Email is the lowest-friction protocol for cross-organization agent communication because it doesn't require both parties to agree on an API contract ahead of time. You just send a message to an address.

The implication: email infrastructure that only thinks about human recipients is going to feel increasingly awkward. Authentication, formatting, even the concept of a "subject line" will evolve when both sender and receiver are machines.

## 2. Prompt injection via email becomes a real attack vector

This one keeps me up at night. Right now, prompt injection in email is mostly theoretical. Security researchers have demonstrated it. A few proof-of-concept attacks have surfaced. But we haven't seen a large-scale incident where an attacker compromised thousands of agents by embedding malicious instructions in email bodies.

By 2027, we will. The math is simple: more agents reading more email means more attack surface. And email is uniquely dangerous because it's an open channel. Anyone can send your agent a message. Unlike API calls (which require authentication) or chat interfaces (which require user interaction), email arrives unsolicited. An attacker doesn't need access to your system. They just need your agent's address.

Gartner's prediction that [over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027](https://www.forbes.com/sites/markminevich/2025/12/31/agentic-ai-takes-over-11-shocking-2026-predictions/) due to "inadequate risk controls" points directly at this. Email injection will be one of the specific risk categories that triggers those cancellations.

The teams that survive will be the ones who built injection scoring into their email pipeline from the start, not bolted it on after an incident. At LobsterMail, we score every inbound message for injection risk before the agent ever sees the content. I think this approach (scan first, deliver second) will become standard by late 2027. Right now, it's still rare.

## 3. Domain reputation becomes an agent-level concern

Today, domain reputation is managed by humans. A marketing team watches their sender score, adjusts sending patterns, removes bounced addresses. Agents don't do any of this. They provision an inbox and start sending.

This works when your agent sends 20 emails a day. It breaks when it sends 2,000. And by 2027, plenty of agents will be sending 2,000+. The ones running outreach, appointment scheduling, customer support, and procurement workflows will burn through sending limits and reputation thresholds that were designed for human pacing.

I predict we'll see the emergence of "reputation-aware" agent email, where the sending infrastructure itself throttles, rotates, and manages domain health without the agent needing to understand deliverability. The agent just says "send this email" and the infrastructure handles warming, backoff, and domain rotation automatically.

This is already partially how we work. Our infrastructure handles SPF, DKIM, and domain authentication so the agent doesn't configure DNS records. But the next step is dynamic reputation management, and I think it'll be table stakes by 2027 for any serious agent email provider.

## 4. Regulatory frameworks will specifically mention agent email

The EU AI Act is already in effect. The US is debating its own frameworks. But none of the current regulations specifically address agent-to-agent email communication, agent email authentication, or the question of who is legally responsible when an agent sends a message.

By 2027, at least one major jurisdiction will publish guidance specifically covering autonomous email senders. The questions are real and unresolved: If an agent sends a fraudulent email, who is liable? The deployer? The model provider? The email infrastructure? Does CAN-SPAM apply to agent-sent messages that don't have a human "sender"?

I don't know what the answers will be, but I'm fairly certain the questions will be formally asked. The volume of agent email will make it impossible for regulators to ignore. Projects building agent email workflows should start thinking about auditability now. Logging who sent what, when, and under whose authority isn't optional when regulators come asking.

## 5. Email becomes the default agent identity layer

Here's the prediction I'm most excited about. Right now, agents authenticate via API keys, OAuth tokens, or session cookies. These all work, but they're invisible to the outside world. An API key doesn't tell another system "this is the procurement agent for Acme Corp."

An email address does. `procurement@acme.com` is both an identity and a communication channel. By 2027, I think we'll see email addresses become the standard way agents identify themselves to external systems, sign up for services, receive confirmations, and establish ongoing relationships with other agents and platforms.

This is already happening with OpenClaw agents. When an agent needs to register for a SaaS tool, it provisions an inbox, signs up with that address, receives a verification email, extracts the code, and confirms. The email address becomes the agent's identity in that system. Multiply this pattern across every service an agent interacts with, and email becomes the identity layer for autonomous AI.

## Two things I'm less sure about

**Will open-source agent email infrastructure emerge?** Maybe. The components exist (Postfix, Haraka, various MTA projects), but the hard part isn't the mail server. It's the anti-abuse systems, the deliverability management, and the injection protection. Building a mail server is a weekend project. Building one that doesn't get blacklisted within a week is a year-long effort. I think most teams will use managed infrastructure rather than self-hosting, but I could be wrong.

**Will email protocols evolve for agents?** SMTP is 43 years old. It works remarkably well, but it wasn't designed for machine-to-machine communication. There's an argument that a new protocol (or at least new extensions to SMTP) could emerge for agent email, with structured payloads, built-in authentication, and injection-resistant formatting. I'd love to see this happen, but protocol changes move slowly. 2027 might be too soon.

## What this means if you're building with agents

If your agent touches email today, or will in the next year, three things matter:

First, choose infrastructure that was built for agents, not adapted from human email services. The security model, the provisioning flow, and the deliverability management are all different when the sender is autonomous.

Second, build injection protection into your email pipeline now. Don't wait for the first headline-making attack. By the time it happens, the damage patterns will already be established.

Third, think about your agent's email address as its identity, not just a communication tool. The address your agent uses will follow it across services, conversations, and potentially regulatory audits. Pick infrastructure that gives your agent a stable, professional identity it can grow into.
