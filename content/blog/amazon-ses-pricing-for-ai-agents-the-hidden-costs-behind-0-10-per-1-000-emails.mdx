Amazon SES charges $0.10 per 1,000 emails. That number appears on every pricing page, every comparison chart, every "cheapest email provider" listicle. And it's technically true. It's also deeply misleading if you're running AI agents that send email autonomously.

I spent two weeks modeling the real cost of running 50 agent inboxes on SES. The per-email rate was the smallest line item on the bill. The actual costs came from places AWS doesn't put in the headline: data transfer fees, deliverability tooling, SNS notifications, dedicated IPs, and the engineering time to build everything SES doesn't include. If you're wondering [what agent email even is](/blog/what-is-agent-email), the short version is this: your AI agent provisions its own inbox, sends and receives mail, and handles bounces and replies without human intervention. SES wasn't built for that workflow, and the bill reflects it.

## Amazon SES hidden costs beyond $0.10 per 1,000 emails

The base rate covers sending. Everything else costs extra:

- **Virtual Deliverability Manager**: $0.07 per 1,000 emails on top of the base rate
- **Data transfer**: $0.12 per GB for outbound data (attachments add up fast)
- **Dedicated IPs**: $24.95/month per address, with a practical minimum of 3-5 for warm-up rotation
- **SNS bounce/complaint notifications**: $0.50 per million requests, plus $0.06 per 100k HTTP deliveries
- **CloudWatch monitoring**: variable, but expect $3-10/month per agent at moderate volume
- **Deliverability dashboard**: $1,250/month for the full dashboard (most agent operators skip this, then regret it)
- **AWS Support plans**: $29/month minimum for developer support, $100+/month for business tier

A solo agent sending 5,000 emails per month might look like a $0.50 SES bill. With Virtual Deliverability Manager, one dedicated IP, SNS notifications, and basic CloudWatch, you're closer to $30-35/month before you've written a line of bounce-handling code.

## The free tier trap

SES offers 3,000 free messages per month if you send from an EC2 instance. That sounds generous until you read the conditions. The free tier only covers *sending*. Receiving emails costs $0.10 per 1,000 incoming messages plus $0.09 per incoming chunk of email data. SNS notifications aren't included. Data transfer isn't included.

AI agents that both send and receive (which is most of them, since agents need to read replies and verification codes) burn through the free tier fast. And the free tier doesn't cover dedicated IPs at all, so your agent is sharing IP reputation with every other SES user on the shared pool. One spammy neighbor tanks your deliverability.

The free tier also expires after 12 months. If you built your agent workflow around "SES is free," you get a surprise bill in month 13.

## Why AI agents accumulate SES costs differently

Human email campaigns are predictable. You schedule a newsletter, send it to a known list, wait for opens. The volume is steady, the recipient list is curated, and bounce rates stay under 2% because someone cleaned the list last quarter.

AI agents don't work like that. They generate recipients dynamically. They retry failed sends. They spin up new sender identities when they need fresh inboxes. They send in bursts (50 emails in 10 seconds, then nothing for an hour) instead of the steady flow that SES rate limits are designed for.

This creates three cost problems that human campaigns never hit:

**Suppression list billing.** SES maintains a global suppression list. If your agent sends to an address on that list, SES still counts it as a sent message and bills you. The email never leaves AWS, but you pay anyway. An agent auto-generating recipient addresses from scraped data can silently waste 10-20% of its send volume on suppressed addresses.

**Bounce rate penalties.** SES suspends your account if bounces exceed 5% or complaints exceed 0.1%. Agents that send to unverified addresses hit these thresholds fast. Getting unsuspended requires a human to file a support case explaining what went wrong and what you've changed. That's not something an autonomous agent can do. We've covered the broader [self-hosted vs managed tradeoffs](/blog/self-hosted-vs-managed-agent-email) before, and bounce handling is one of the biggest hidden costs in the self-hosted column.

**Rate limit infrastructure.** SES starts new accounts at 200 emails per day with a 1 email/second send rate. You can request increases, but warm-up takes weeks. Agents that need to send immediately hit the quota wall, which means you need queuing infrastructure (SQS, Lambda, retry logic) to buffer outbound messages. That's more AWS services, more cost, more code to maintain.

## The engineering cost nobody counts

SES is a sending API. It's not an email platform. To build what an AI agent actually needs, you're assembling parts from across AWS:

- **SES** for sending
- **S3** for storing received emails
- **SNS** for bounce and complaint notifications
- **Lambda** for processing incoming mail
- **SQS** for queuing and retry logic
- **Route 53** for DNS (SPF, DKIM, DMARC records)
- **CloudWatch** for monitoring and alerting
- **IAM** for access control per agent

Each service has its own pricing model, its own configuration surface, and its own failure modes. The monthly AWS bill for this stack, running 50 agents, lands somewhere between $150-400/month depending on volume. But the real cost is the 40-80 hours of engineering time to build and maintain the glue code that connects all of it.

If you're [running 50 agent inboxes](/blog/running-50-agent-inboxes), that engineering overhead matters more than the per-email rate. The $0.10 per 1,000 emails is a rounding error compared to the cost of building inbox provisioning, bounce handling, reputation monitoring, and injection protection from scratch.

## How SES compares to purpose-built agent email

SES is optimized for marketing teams sending newsletters and transactional receipts. It's very good at that. The pricing makes sense when a human is managing one verified domain, one sender identity, and a curated recipient list.

Agent email is a different problem. Agents need to self-provision inboxes without human intervention. They need to receive mail, not just send it. They need protection from prompt injection attacks hiding in email bodies. They need to handle bounces programmatically, not through a support ticket.

LobsterMail was built specifically for this workflow. An agent installs the SDK, calls `LobsterMail.create()`, and gets a working inbox in seconds. No DNS configuration, no warm-up period, no IAM roles, no SNS topic subscriptions. The free tier includes 1,000 emails per month with both sending and receiving. The Builder tier at $9/month covers most production agent workloads.

The comparison isn't really about per-email pricing. It's about total cost of ownership: what you pay AWS, what you pay in engineering hours, and what you pay when an agent burns a domain because nobody built the bounce-handling logic.

## When SES actually makes sense

I don't think SES is a bad product. If you're sending 100,000+ marketing emails per month from a single domain with a dedicated ops team managing deliverability, SES is probably the cheapest option available. The per-email rate at high volume is genuinely hard to beat.

But for AI agents that need self-service inbox provisioning, low-volume transactional sending, and built-in safety rails, SES adds complexity and cost that the headline price doesn't reveal. The $0.10 per 1,000 number is accurate. The total cost of running agent email on SES is not $0.10 per 1,000.

Know what you're paying for before you architect around a price that only tells part of the story.
