Your agent fires off an outbound email and gets back a `550` error. The message didn't land in spam. It didn't get delayed. The receiving server looked at it and said *no*.

A 550 is a permanent rejection. Unlike a 4xx temporary failure (where retrying might work), a 550 tells your SMTP client that this message will never be accepted in its current form. For AI agents running automated workflows, a single 550 can stall an entire pipeline. A pattern of them can get your sending account restricted, your domain flagged, or your IP blacklisted.

The fix depends on which 550 sub-code you're seeing. Let me walk through the common ones and what to do about each.

## What a 550 error actually means

Every email rejection includes an SMTP response code. The `5` means permanent failure. The `50` narrows it to the mailbox or address. But the sub-code after that tells you *why* it failed, and different sub-codes require completely different fixes.

Here are the ones agents hit most often:

- **550 5.1.1** or **550 5.1.2**: The recipient address doesn't exist. Your agent is sending to a bad address.
- **550 5.1.8**: "Access denied, bad outbound sender." Microsoft 365 has flagged your sending account as compromised or spammy and blocked outbound mail entirely.
- **550 5.7.1**: Authentication failed. The receiving server requires SPF, DKIM, or DMARC alignment and your message didn't pass.
- **550 5.7.25**: The sending IP has no PTR record, or the PTR doesn't match the sending domain.
- **550 5.7.26**: SPF alignment failure specifically. Your envelope sender doesn't match your SPF record.

The sub-code is everything. Without it, you're guessing.

## Why agents trigger 550 errors differently than humans

A human sending 20 emails a day from Outlook almost never sees a 550. An agent sending 200 emails an hour from a programmatic pipeline sees them constantly. Here's why.

**Volume without warmup.** Agents don't ease into sending. They go from zero to full throughput the moment they're deployed. Receiving servers interpret sudden volume from an unknown sender as spam, and respond with 550 rejections before even reading the message content. We covered the warmup process in detail in our guide on [building an AI sales outreach agent that doesn't get blacklisted](/blog/ai-sales-outreach-agent-email).

**Shared IP contamination.** If your agent sends through a shared email provider, its messages travel from the same IP pool as every other customer. One bad actor on that pool can tank the IP's reputation, causing your agent's legitimate messages to get 550'd at the door.

**Stale recipient lists.** Agents processing CRM queues or scraping contact data often send to addresses that haven't been verified recently. High bounce rates (anything over 2%) trigger automated blocks on most major providers.

**Missing or broken authentication.** This is the most common cause I see. The agent's sending domain has no SPF record, or the SPF record doesn't include the sending infrastructure's IP range. DKIM isn't configured, so the message arrives unsigned. DMARC is set to `p=reject` on the receiving end, and your unauthenticated message gets bounced. Our [email deliverability guide](/blog/email-deliverability-ai-agents) walks through the full SPF, DKIM, and DMARC setup.

## How to fix a 550 outbound email rejection

A 550 rejection means the receiving server permanently refused your email. The fix depends on the specific sub-code, but here's a systematic approach:

1. **Read the full NDR (non-delivery report) and identify the sub-code.** The bounce message contains the exact 550 variant. Don't skip this. `550 5.1.8` and `550 5.7.1` have entirely different causes.
2. **Check if your sending account is restricted.** In Microsoft 365, go to the Microsoft Defender portal > Review > Restricted Entities. If your account is listed, unblock it before doing anything else.
3. **Verify SPF, DKIM, and DMARC alignment for your sending domain.** Use `dig TXT yourdomain.com` to check your SPF record. Confirm it includes your sending provider's IP range. Verify DKIM signing is active and DMARC is set to at least `p=none` during testing.
4. **Review transport and connection filter rules.** Overly broad outbound rules in Exchange Online or your MTA can silently block categories of messages. Check for rules that match on sender, subject, or content patterns your agent's messages might trigger.
5. **Validate your recipient list.** Run a verification pass on all target addresses before sending. Remove any address that returned a 550 5.1.1 (user unknown) in the past 30 days.
6. **Check your sending IP's reputation.** Look up your outbound IP on Spamhaus, Barracuda, and Microsoft SNDS. If it's listed, submit a delisting request and investigate what triggered the listing.
7. **Reduce send volume and implement gradual warmup.** If you're on a new domain or IP, cut volume to 10-20 emails per day and ramp up over 4-6 weeks.

## The 550 5.1.8 problem in Microsoft 365

This one deserves its own section because it's so common and so poorly documented.

When Microsoft 365 detects suspicious outbound activity from an account, it restricts the account and returns `550 5.1.8 Access denied, bad outbound sender` on every subsequent message. The account isn't deleted or suspended. It's quarantined. But the error message doesn't tell you that.

To fix it:

1. Sign into the Microsoft Defender portal at `security.microsoft.com`.
2. Navigate to Review > Restricted Entities.
3. Select the blocked account and click Unblock.
4. Change the account password (Microsoft requires this).
5. Enable multi-factor authentication if it's not already active.

After unblocking, the restriction lifts within an hour for most accounts. But if the underlying cause (compromised credentials, automated spam, or excessive bounce rates) isn't addressed, the account will get re-restricted within days.

For agents running on Microsoft infrastructure, this is a recurring headache. The detection heuristics are tuned for human email patterns, and automated senders trip them regularly.

## Monitoring your agent's outbound health

Catching 550 errors after they've cascaded into a domain block is too late. You want to detect them in real time.

At minimum, log every SMTP response code from your agent's outbound sends. Set an alert threshold: if more than 1% of sends in any rolling hour return a 5xx code, pause outbound mail and investigate. Most email APIs return the full SMTP response in their error payload.

Track these three metrics daily:

- **Bounce rate** by sub-code (separate hard bounces from soft bounces)
- **Spam complaint rate** (target under 0.1%, hard ceiling at 0.3%)
- **Delivery success rate** broken down by recipient domain

If you see 550 5.7.1 errors concentrated at Gmail recipients but nowhere else, your DKIM setup is probably broken for that specific sending path. If 550 5.1.1 errors spike across all domains, your recipient data has gone stale. The pattern tells you where to look.

## Don't let 550 errors become 550 habits

A single 550 is a fixable problem. A pattern of 550s is a reputation problem, and reputation takes weeks to rebuild.

The fastest path to clean outbound delivery: verify your authentication records, validate your recipient lists before sending, warm up new domains gradually, and monitor bounce codes in real time. If your agent is sending from shared infrastructure where you can't control the IP reputation, that's the first thing to change.
