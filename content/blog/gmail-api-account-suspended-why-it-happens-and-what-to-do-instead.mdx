You're building an automation that sends emails through the Gmail API. Everything works in development. You push to production, run it for a few days, and then one morning you open your browser to find: "This account has been suspended."

No warning. No grace period. Your personal Google account, with years of Drive files, Photos, and contacts, is locked behind an abuse review. The automation that triggered it? Maybe 300 emails a day. Maybe fewer.

This happens more often than Google's documentation would suggest. Threads on Reddit and Stack Overflow are full of developers who lost access to their entire Google account after using the Gmail API in ways that looked perfectly reasonable to them. One [r/GoogleAppsScript user](https://www.reddit.com/r/GoogleAppsScript/comments/1fm98pg/account_suspended_for_abuse/) reported a suspension despite staying well within documented quotas. A [Stack Overflow question](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/42643081/testing-the-outlook-rest-api-leads-to-suspended-account-issue) describes the same pattern with Outlook's API, suggesting the problem isn't unique to Google.

The core issue isn't the API itself. It's that consumer email accounts were never designed for programmatic use, and the abuse detection systems treat automated patterns as threats.

## Why the Gmail API causes account suspensions

Google's abuse detection doesn't just count emails. It analyzes behavioral patterns. Your account can get suspended for Gmail API usage that triggers any of these signals:

1. Sending more than 500 emails per day on a free Gmail account (2,000 for Workspace)
2. Rapid API call bursts that look like bot activity
3. Sending identical or near-identical content to many recipients
4. High bounce rates from invalid recipient addresses
5. Recipients marking your messages as spam
6. Sudden volume spikes from an account with no sending history

The frustrating part is that Google doesn't tell you which signal you tripped. The suspension message is generic: "This account has been suspended due to a violation of our Terms of Service." You're left guessing.

And here's what makes it worse for developers: the suspension often applies to your entire Google account, not just the Gmail API access. Your Drive, Calendar, YouTube, and every other Google service goes down with it. If you were using a personal account for development (which most solo developers do), that's years of data behind a locked door.

## What happens when your Gmail account is suspended

When Google suspends your account, the effects are immediate:

Your API calls start returning `403` errors with a message about the account being disabled. Any OAuth tokens you've issued stop working. If you're running an AI agent or automation pipeline, every downstream process that depends on email breaks at once.

You can't send or receive email. You can't access any Google service tied to that account. If other applications use "Sign in with Google" through that account, those stop working too.

The suspension review process is slow. Google's [account recovery form](https://accounts.google.com/signin/recovery) is the starting point, but responses can take days or weeks. Some developers report never getting their accounts back. Others get reinstated after a manual review, only to be suspended again when they resume the same API usage patterns.

## How to file a reinstatement request

If your account has already been suspended, here's what to do:

Go to [accounts.google.com/signin/recovery](https://accounts.google.com/signin/recovery) and follow the prompts. Be specific about what you were building and why your usage was legitimate. If you have a Google Workspace account, contact your admin first since they can sometimes resolve it faster through the admin console.

While waiting, don't create a new account and immediately resume the same API usage. Google's detection systems can link accounts, and you'll end up with two suspended accounts instead of one.

## Rate-limiting won't save you

The standard advice is to add rate limiting to your Gmail API calls. That helps with quota errors (HTTP 429), but it doesn't prevent abuse suspensions. Google's abuse detection looks at patterns over days and weeks, not just instantaneous request rates.

You can follow every best practice in the book: implement exponential backoff, respect the `Retry-After` header, batch your requests, spread sends across the day. And you might still get flagged if the overall pattern looks automated. Because it is automated. That's the whole point.

The Gmail API's [daily usage quotas](https://developers.google.com/gmail/api/reference/quota) give you a specific number: 250 quota units per user per second, with different operations costing different amounts. But staying under these quotas doesn't mean you won't get suspended. The abuse detection system operates independently of the quota system.

## Google Apps Script vs. the Gmail API

Some developers switch to Google Apps Script as a workaround, and it does seem to trigger fewer suspensions for light usage. Apps Script has its own quota system (separate from the Gmail API) and runs within Google's own infrastructure, which may make automated usage look less suspicious.

But the limits are tight. Free accounts get 100 email recipients per day through Apps Script. Workspace accounts get 1,500. And you're still running on Google's infrastructure, subject to the same Terms of Service. If your agent needs to send or receive hundreds of emails daily, Apps Script isn't a real solution. It's a temporary reprieve.

## The real problem: consumer email accounts aren't infrastructure

The Gmail API is an interface to a consumer email product. Google built it so apps can access a user's mailbox, not so developers can run email infrastructure on top of Gmail's servers.

When you use the Gmail API for automated sending, you're essentially asking Google to let you use a free consumer product as transactional email infrastructure. Google's response to that is predictable: abuse detection, suspensions, and a recovery process designed for humans who got hacked, not developers who were building automations.

This is especially painful for AI agents. An agent that provisions its own email, signs up for services, and processes incoming messages is exactly the usage pattern Google's abuse systems are designed to block. The agent isn't doing anything malicious, but from Google's perspective, it looks identical to a compromised account being used for spam.

## What to use instead

If your automation or AI agent needs email, you have a few options that don't involve risking your Google account.

**Transactional email services** like SendGrid, Postmark, or Mailgun are built for programmatic sending. They expect automated usage and won't suspend you for it (as long as you follow their anti-spam policies). The downside: they handle sending only, not receiving. And they require human signup, API key management, and domain verification.

**Self-hosted email** (Postfix, Haraka, etc.) gives you full control. The downside: you're now responsible for deliverability, IP reputation, SPF/DKIM/DMARC configuration, and ongoing maintenance. That's a full-time job in itself.

## Audit your current setup

If you're currently using the Gmail API for automated email, ask yourself these questions before you get suspended:

Are you using a personal Gmail account or a Workspace account? Personal accounts have lower thresholds for everything. Is your sending volume growing? Abuse detection is more sensitive to spikes than steady volume. Are you sending similar content to many recipients? Even if it's legitimate, it looks like spam to automated systems. Do you have a backup plan? If your Google account gets suspended tomorrow, what breaks?

The answer to that last question is usually "everything." And that's exactly why tying automated email to a consumer account is risky. The cost of a suspension isn't just lost email access. It's losing your entire Google identity.

If you're running an AI agent that needs reliable email, use infrastructure built for agents. Your Google account will thank you.
