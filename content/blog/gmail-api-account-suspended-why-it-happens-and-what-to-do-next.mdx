A developer on r/GoogleAppsScript posted a familiar horror story last year. Their script had been running fine for months, sending emails through the Gmail API without issues. Then one morning: account suspended for abuse. No warning. No specific violation cited. Just a locked account and a support form that leads to an auto-reply.

This isn't rare. Search "account suspended gmail api" and you'll find Stack Overflow threads, Reddit posts, and GitHub issues from developers who hit Google's abuse detection systems without understanding what triggered them. The Outlook REST API has similar problems (one developer on Stack Overflow reported getting suspended just from *testing*), but Gmail's enforcement is particularly opaque because Google rarely explains which specific behavior crossed the line.

If you're building automation or AI agents that rely on Gmail API access, you need to understand why this happens, how to recover, and whether Gmail API is the right foundation for programmatic email in the first place.

## Why does the Gmail API suspend accounts?

Google's abuse detection evaluates multiple signals simultaneously. A suspension usually results from tripping several of these at once:

1. **Exceeding daily sending quotas.** Free Gmail accounts are limited to roughly 500 emails per day. Google Workspace accounts get 2,000. These aren't hard ceilings you can safely approach; sending near the limit consistently flags your account.
2. **Sudden volume spikes.** Going from 10 emails a day to 400 overnight looks like a compromised account to Google's systems, even if every email is legitimate.
3. **High bounce rates.** Sending to invalid addresses signals spam behavior. If more than 5-10% of your messages bounce, you're in danger.
4. **Recipient complaints.** Even a small number of "Report spam" clicks from recipients can trigger a review.
5. **Repetitive or templated content.** Sending identical or near-identical messages to many recipients matches spam patterns.
6. **Suspicious OAuth behavior.** Requesting broad scopes, authenticating from many different IPs, or cycling through multiple accounts raises flags.
7. **Violating Google's Acceptable Use Policy.** This is the catch-all. Google defines "abuse" broadly, and their enforcement is automated first, reviewed later (if at all).

The frustrating part is that Google doesn't tell you which of these triggered the suspension. You get a generic "suspended for abuse" message and a link to an appeal form.

## What happens when your Google account is suspended

This is where it gets serious. A Gmail API suspension isn't just about losing API access. Depending on the type of suspension, you can lose access to your entire Google account: Gmail, Drive, Calendar, Photos, everything tied to that Google identity.

For personal accounts, Google may lock you out completely. Your emails become inaccessible. Any services using that Google account for authentication stop working. If you were using that account as your primary email, you've also lost your recovery address for dozens of other services.

For Google Workspace accounts, the admin can usually restore access, but the suspended user's email stops flowing during the suspension period. Inbound messages may bounce, which damages your domain's reputation with other mail servers.

The timeline for resolution varies wildly. Some developers report getting reinstated within 24 hours after filing an appeal. Others describe weeks of back-and-forth with automated responses, never reaching a human reviewer. A few never got their accounts back at all.

## How to check your Gmail API quota usage

Before you hit a suspension, monitor where you stand. The Google Cloud Console shows your current API usage under **APIs & Services > Gmail API > Quotas**. You can see daily sending limits, per-user rate limits, and how close you are to each ceiling.

You can also query quota usage programmatically through the Cloud Monitoring API, but most developers don't set this up until after their first suspension. Set up alerts at 70% and 90% of your daily quota. If your agent is approaching those thresholds, throttle it before Google does it for you.

For Google Apps Script specifically, quotas are documented at [developers.google.com/apps-script/guides/services/quotas](https://developers.google.com/apps-script/guides/services/quotas), but the numbers listed there are maximums, not safe operating ranges. Running at 80% of a listed quota for consecutive days is enough to trigger a review.

## How to recover a suspended account

If you're already suspended, here's the process:

**Step 1: Check the suspension type.** Go to [accounts.google.com](https://accounts.google.com) and look for the specific message. "Suspended for abuse" and "disabled for a violation of our Terms of Service" have different appeal paths.

**Step 2: File an appeal immediately.** Use the form Google links to in the suspension notice. Be specific about what your application does, what volume it sends, and why the usage is legitimate. Vague appeals get vague rejections.

**Step 3: Reduce or stop all API activity on related accounts.** If you're running the same application across multiple Google accounts, pause everything. Google's systems can link accounts, and continued activity on related accounts can delay your appeal.

**Step 4: Wait.** Google's appeal process is not fast. Expect 3-7 business days for a first response, and that response is often automated. If rejected, you can appeal again with more detail, but each round takes days.

**Step 5: If you have a Google Workspace account, contact your reseller or Google support directly.** Paid accounts get better support access than free accounts. This is one area where paying for Workspace genuinely helps.

For Google Workspace admins specifically: you can restore a suspended user from the Admin Console under **Directory > Users**, find the suspended user, and click "Reactivate." But this only works for internal admin suspensions, not for abuse suspensions initiated by Google's automated systems.

## Can you use Gmail API for high-volume programmatic email?

Technically, yes. Practically, it's risky. Gmail API was designed for applications that act on behalf of a human user checking their inbox, not for autonomous systems sending hundreds of transactional emails. Google's terms reflect this: they explicitly prohibit using Gmail for "bulk email sending" and define the boundary loosely enough that any automated sending at scale could qualify.

Google does offer sandbox environments for development through the Gmail API's test mode, but these are limited. There's no official staging environment that mirrors production behavior without counting against your quotas. This means even *testing* your email automation counts toward the same limits that can get you suspended.

If your use case involves an AI agent that needs to send and receive email autonomously, you're fighting the Gmail API's design assumptions. It assumes a human is in the loop. It assumes sending patterns will look like a person using email, not a script processing a queue.
