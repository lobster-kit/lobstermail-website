NanoClaw showed up about six months ago as a lighter, more locked-down alternative to OpenClaw. It runs each skill in its own Docker container, keeps a full audit log of every action, and follows a "skills over features" model where you bolt on only what you need. That last part is exactly why email is interesting here: NanoClaw doesn't ship with email built in. You choose how your agent gets it.

And the way you choose matters more than you'd think. Gmail will suspend agent accounts without warning (the [r/AI_Agents PSA thread](https://www.reddit.com/r/AI_Agents/) from January made that painfully clear). Mailgun and Postmark require human signup, DNS records, and domain verification before your agent can send a single message. For an architecture that's supposed to let you add capabilities with a single command, that's a lot of manual steps.

LobsterMail was built for exactly this situation. Your agent provisions its own inbox, no human signup required, and the NanoClaw skill handles authentication and error recovery out of the box. If you want to skip the background and jump straight to setup, <InlineGetStarted>get your NanoClaw agent its own inbox here</InlineGetStarted> and paste the instructions into your agent's chat.

## How to connect LobsterMail to NanoClaw (step-by-step)

This is the full integration path, from zero to working email:

1. Install the LobsterMail skill from the skills marketplace into your NanoClaw instance.
2. Tell your NanoClaw agent in natural language to provision its own inbox.
3. The agent auto-signs up, stores the token, and creates a `@lobstermail.ai` address.
4. Configure the skill as a "tool" (agent sends email when triggered) or a "channel" (inbound email wakes the agent).
5. Test by sending an email to your agent's new address and confirming it processes the message.
6. Optionally set up a webhook so inbound mail triggers your agent in real time instead of polling.

That's it. No DNS records to configure. No OAuth flows to complete. No API keys to copy-paste between dashboards. The agent handles provisioning itself because that's what LobsterMail was designed for.

## What NanoClaw actually is (and why email matters)

NanoClaw is a lightweight, self-hosted AI assistant framework created by the Cohen brothers at Qwibit. It came out of frustration with OpenClaw's monolithic architecture, where every user inherits the security surface of every module whether they use it or not.

The core idea is container isolation. Each skill runs in its own Docker sandbox with explicit permissions. If your agent only needs WhatsApp and email, it doesn't carry the attack surface of fifty other integrations. [VentureBeat covered the architecture](https://venturebeat.com/orchestration/nanoclaw-solves-one-of-openclaws-biggest-security-issues-and-its-already) in detail, noting that Qwibit's own AI agency runs on NanoClaw internally.

Email is one of the most requested capabilities for any agent framework. Agents need to sign up for services, receive verification codes, send notifications, and communicate with humans. But email is also one of the riskiest integrations from a security perspective. Every inbound email is an attack vector for prompt injection. Every outbound email sent from a compromised agent can damage your domain reputation permanently.

NanoClaw's sandboxed approach helps with the containment side. LobsterMail helps with the email-specific risks: injection scoring on every inbound message, rate limiting to prevent runaway sends, and audit-friendly logging that plays well with NanoClaw's own auditability features.

## NanoClaw vs. OpenClaw: how email integration differs

OpenClaw's `add-gmail` skill connects directly to your personal Gmail account. Your agent reads your inbox, sends from your address, and operates under your Google account's identity. This works until Google's automated systems flag the unusual access patterns (high-frequency API calls, programmatic login from containers, bulk operations) and suspend your account. The [Reddit PSA](https://www.reddit.com/r/AI_Agents/) about Gmail nuking OpenClaw agents without warning wasn't an isolated incident. It's a predictable consequence of using consumer email infrastructure for agent workloads.

NanoClaw took a different path. Instead of hardcoding a Gmail integration, it uses the skills marketplace to let you choose your email provider. The `add-gmail` skill exists on the LobeHub marketplace, but the same risks apply. Google doesn't distinguish between "human using Gmail" and "agent using Gmail." It just sees behavior that looks automated and acts accordingly.

With LobsterMail, your agent gets its own identity. A dedicated `@lobstermail.ai` address (or your own custom domain) that was designed for programmatic use from day one. No shared credentials with your personal accounts. No risk of a suspension taking down your human email along with your agent's.

The practical difference: when an OpenClaw agent's Gmail gets banned, the human loses access to their personal email too. When a NanoClaw agent's LobsterMail inbox hits a rate limit, the agent gets a clear error message and the human's accounts are completely unaffected.

## What the Docker sandbox means for email routing

NanoClaw runs skills inside Docker containers. This raises a legitimate question: can email actually reach an agent that's sandboxed?

The answer depends on how you configure the LobsterMail skill. There are two modes:

**Tool mode.** Your agent calls LobsterMail's API when it needs to check or send email. The SDK makes outbound HTTPS requests to LobsterMail's servers, which works from inside any Docker container with network access. Inbound emails are stored on LobsterMail's servers until your agent polls for them with `inbox.receive()`. No special networking configuration required.

**Channel mode.** Inbound email triggers your agent automatically. This uses LobsterMail webhooks, where LobsterMail sends an HTTP POST to a URL your NanoClaw instance exposes. For self-hosted NanoClaw deployments, this means your server needs a publicly accessible endpoint (or a tunnel like ngrok during development). NanoClaw's built-in routing handles passing the webhook payload to the correct containerized skill.

Most NanoClaw users start with tool mode because it works out of the box. Channel mode is better for agents that need to respond to email in real time, like a support agent or an appointment scheduler.

## Security considerations

NanoClaw's audit log captures every action a skill takes. When the LobsterMail skill sends an email, that send is logged with the full payload, timestamp, and result. When it reads an email, the read event is logged too. This gives you a complete record of your agent's email activity without building custom logging.

On the inbound side, LobsterMail scores every email for prompt injection risk before your agent processes it. This is relevant for NanoClaw because the Docker sandbox isolates runtime damage but doesn't prevent an agent from being manipulated by a carefully crafted email. Injection scoring adds a layer of protection at the content level that complements NanoClaw's container-level isolation.

The combination, sandboxed execution plus injection-scored email, is stronger than either approach alone.

## When LobsterMail isn't the right fit

If your NanoClaw agent only needs to send transactional email at high volume (thousands per day) and you already have a Mailgun or Postmark account with a warmed domain, those services will give you higher sending limits at the transactional tier. LobsterMail's Builder plan caps at 500 sends per day, which covers most agent workflows but won't work for a mass-notification system.

If your agent needs to read a specific human's existing inbox (like processing a founder's personal email), LobsterMail isn't designed for that. You'd need OAuth-based access to the human's account, with all the risks that entails.

For everything else, the self-provisioning model saves enough setup time to be worth trying on the free tier first.
