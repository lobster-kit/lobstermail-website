There are at least six protocols competing to become the standard way AI agents talk to each other. MCP, A2A, ACP, ANP, AP2, and a handful of others launched in the past year alone. Each promises to solve inter-agent communication with a new specification, a new handshake, a new schema.

Meanwhile, email has been routing messages between autonomous systems since 1971.

I'm not saying the new protocols are pointless. Some of them solve real problems. But the conversation around inter-agent communication tends to ignore the protocol that already has universal reach, built-in authentication, and zero adoption friction. So let's talk about email as an inter-agent communication protocol, why it works better than you'd expect, and where it falls short.

## The protocol explosion

The past twelve months have been busy. Google released A2A (Agent-to-Agent) for structured agent collaboration. Anthropic's MCP (Model Context Protocol) became the go-to for giving agents access to tools. AWS published a blog series on using MCP for inter-agent communication. Twilio announced A2H for agent-to-human channels. IBM and BeeAI pushed ACP for multi-agent coordination across frameworks.

Each protocol targets a slightly different slice of the problem. MCP is about tool access and context sharing within an organization. A2A handles structured task delegation between agents. ACP focuses on cross-framework compatibility so agents built with different SDKs can collaborate.

They're all useful. They're also all new, which means limited adoption, evolving specs, and the very real possibility that half of them won't exist in two years. If you've built integrations on top of abandoned protocols before, you know the feeling.

## What email already does

Email is a message-passing protocol with 50+ years of battle testing. When you strip away the human-centric UI (inbox, folders, reply threads), what you're left with is a reliable, asynchronous, globally-routed messaging system. That's exactly what inter-agent communication needs.

Here's what email gives you out of the box:

**Universal addressing.** Every agent can have an address. Every server, every service, every SaaS product already accepts email. Your agent doesn't need the other party to install a library, support a protocol, or open a port. If they have an email address, your agent can reach them.

**Authentication built in.** SPF, DKIM, and DMARC have been solving sender verification for over a decade. When your agent receives an email, it can cryptographically verify who sent it. That's not a solved problem in most new protocols yet. Twilio's A2H announcement specifically highlights the need for "cryptographic evidence" in agent interactions. Email has had that since 2011.

**Asynchronous by default.** Agents don't always respond instantly. Email handles this naturally. Messages queue, retry on temporary failures, and deliver when the recipient is ready. No websocket connections to maintain, no heartbeat checks, no timeout handling.

**Human-readable audit trail.** Every message has headers, timestamps, routing information, and a body that a human can read if something goes wrong. When your agent-to-agent workflow breaks at 3am, you can open the email and see exactly what was sent.

**Firewall-friendly.** Email passes through corporate firewalls, government networks, and locked-down environments that would block custom protocol traffic. SMTP on port 25/587 is allowed almost everywhere.

## Where email falls short (honestly)

Email isn't perfect for every inter-agent scenario. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise.

**Latency.** Email delivery typically takes 1 to 30 seconds. For real-time collaboration where agents need sub-second responses, that's too slow. If two agents are co-writing code in a tight loop, email isn't the right channel. MCP or A2A over HTTP would be better.

**Structured data.** Email bodies are text (or HTML). You can put JSON in them, and agents can parse it, but there's no schema enforcement at the protocol level. ACP and A2A define typed message schemas that prevent malformed payloads. Email relies on the sender and receiver agreeing on format.

**Discovery.** How does Agent A find Agent B's email address? Email doesn't have a built-in service registry. New protocols like A2A include agent discovery mechanisms (agent cards, capability advertisements). With email, you need a separate directory or hardcoded addresses.

**Bandwidth.** Sending a 50MB dataset between agents over email is technically possible but painful. Attachment limits, encoding overhead, and relay restrictions make email a poor choice for bulk data transfer.

These are real limitations. For tightly-coupled agent systems running on the same infrastructure, a purpose-built protocol will outperform email on speed and structure.

## The case for email as the default

But most agent communication isn't tightly coupled. Most of it looks like this: Agent A needs to tell Agent B something, get a response eventually, and have a record of the exchange. That's a message. Email handles messages.

Consider the practical scenarios. An agent signing up for a service needs to receive a verification code. An agent coordinating with another team's agent needs to send a request and wait for approval. An agent monitoring a system needs to notify another agent (or a human) when something breaks.

In every one of these cases, the other party already supports email. They don't support ACP. They probably don't support A2A. They might support MCP if they're running a compatible stack.

Email works today, with everyone, without coordination. That's not a small advantage. That's the entire game for agents operating in the real world rather than in a controlled multi-agent sandbox.

## A hybrid approach makes sense

The smart play isn't email OR new protocols. It's email as the universal fallback with specialized protocols for high-performance agent-to-agent workflows.

Use MCP when your agent needs to access tools and context within your own infrastructure. Use A2A when you're building a multi-agent pipeline where all participants run compatible stacks. Use email for everything that touches the outside world, because the outside world already speaks SMTP.

This is similar to how humans communicate. We use Slack for real-time team chat, but email for cross-organization communication. The reason is the same: email doesn't require the other party to be on your platform.

For agents, the pattern holds. Internal coordination can use whatever protocol fits best. External communication, interacting with services, other organizations' agents, and legacy systems, should use email because it's the one channel guaranteed to work.

## What this looks like in practice

An agent that needs to communicate externally just needs an inbox. With LobsterMail, the agent provisions its own email address without any human setup, then sends and receives like any other email participant. The agent gets a real address, real authentication (SPF, DKIM), and real delivery.

The receiving party doesn't need to know or care that they're communicating with an agent. The email arrives, gets processed, and a response goes back. No protocol negotiation, no capability exchange, no version compatibility checks.

That simplicity is the point. The best inter-agent communication protocol for external interactions is the one that requires zero coordination with the other side.

## Where this is heading

I think we'll see convergence. A2A and ACP will mature and find their niches for structured multi-agent orchestration. MCP will remain dominant for tool access. And email will persist as the universal transport layer, the protocol agents use when they need to reach anything outside their immediate ecosystem.

The agents that will be most capable aren't the ones locked into a single protocol. They're the ones that can pick the right channel for each interaction. Sometimes that's a typed RPC call over A2A. Sometimes it's a plain email.

If you're building an agent that needs to interact with the outside world, giving it an email address is the fastest path to universal connectivity. You can always add specialized protocols later for specific high-performance use cases. But email gives your agent a communication channel that works with every service, every organization, and every other agent on the internet, right now.
